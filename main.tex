\section{The Proof}
We wish to prove that $\sqrt{2}$ is an irrational number.

\begin{proof}
Suppose for the sake of contradiction that $\sqrt{2}$ is rational. Then it can be expressed as a fraction in lowest terms:
\[
\sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b}
\]
where $a$ and $b$ are integers with no common factors other than $1$, and $b \neq 0$. Squaring both sides gives:
\[
2 = \frac{a^2}{b^2} \implies a^2 = 2b^2
\]
This implies that $a^2$ is even, which means $a$ must also be even. Let $a = 2k$ for some integer $k$. Substituting this back in:
\[
(2k)^2 = 2b^2 \implies 4k^2 = 2b^2 \implies b^2 = 2k^2
\]
This means $b^2$ is also even, so $b$ must be even as well. However, this contradicts our initial assumption that $a$ and $b$ share no common factors! Therefore, $\sqrt{2}$ cannot be rational!
\[
\sqrt{2} \notin \mathbb{Q} \quad \blacksquare
\]
\end{proof}
